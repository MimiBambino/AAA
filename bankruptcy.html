Consumer Bankruptcy

Bankruptcy gives people a fresh start by relieving burdensome debts. Nearly all consumers who file a bankruptcy case file under either Chapter 7 or Chapter 13.

Chapter 7

In a Chapter 7 bankruptcy the debtorâ€™s property is divided into exempt and nonexempt assets. The debtor will keep the exempt assets. The nonexempt assets become part of the bankruptcy estate. The bankruptcy estate is liquidated and the proceeds paid to creditors. Once the bankruptcy process is complete, the debtor is usually released from personal liability for his or her debts. There are income restrictions that apply to Chapter 7 cases. If a debtor's income is too high, he or she will not qualify for Chapter 7.

Chapter 13

In a Chapter 13 bankruptcy the debtor proposes a repayment plan and repays some or all of the debts owed. If a debtor is behind on a mortgage and wants to keep the home, he or she will need to file a Chapter 13 case in order to repay the past due amount on the mortgage. Many consumers file a Chapter 13 case because they do not qualify for Chapter 7. There are debt limits that cannot be exceeded in order to qualify for Chapter 13. If a consumer's debt exceeds these limits the debtor could still qualify for Chapter 11.

Protect Your Financial Future

When used correctly, bankruptcy is a powerful tool that can relieve a consumer of liability for debts that the debtor can't pay back. This gives the debtor the opportunity to start over and build a new, more positive financial future and credit history.
